Overview

In this assessment, you will conduct a complete data analysis project — from identifying a dataset and formulating a research question to statistical modelling and communication of findings. This is a capstone project that draws on all of the skills developed throughout the semester: data acquisition, cleaning, visualisation, descriptive analysis, and regression modelling.

Weight: 40% of final grade

Length: 1,500 words (as measured in the Quarto source document, excluding code)

Task

You will find your own dataset on a topic that interests you, formulate a research question, and answer it using one or more regression models. Your analysis should go beyond description and investigate the relationship between an outcome of interest and one or more predictor variables.

Requirements

  1. Choose a dataset. Find a publicly available dataset relevant to social science research. The dataset must:

    • Contain at least 200 observations.
    • Include at least one plausible outcome variable (continuous or binary) and two or more potential predictor variables.
    • Be available in a format you can import into R (e.g., .csv, .sav, .xlsx, .dta, .rds).

  2. Formulate a research question. Your research question should be clear, specific, and answerable with the data you have chosen. It should concern the relationship between variables — not simply describe a single variable.

  3. Prepare the data. Import, inspect, clean, and recode your variables as necessary. Handle missing values appropriately and justify your decisions.

  4. Fit at least one regression model. Depending on your outcome variable, fit an appropriate model:

    • If your outcome is continuous, use linear regression (lm()).
    • If your outcome is binary, use logistic regression (glm() with family = binomial).

  5. Present and interpret your results. Report coefficients, standard errors, and measures of model fit. Interpret your findings in substantive terms.

ImportantChoosing a good dataset

Pick a dataset and a question that genuinely interest you — your engagement with the topic will show in the quality of your analysis. Avoid datasets that are already heavily cleaned and packaged as textbook exercises (e.g., mtcars, iris). The goal is to demonstrate your ability to work with real-world data.

Report structure

Your report should include the following sections.

1. Introduction

Introduce your research question and explain why it is interesting or relevant. Briefly describe the dataset: its source, what it covers, the unit of observation, and the number of observations.

2. Data and variables

For each variable (outcome and predictors):

  • Describe what the variable measures.
  • Report descriptive statistics (e.g., mean, standard deviation, frequency distributions as appropriate).
  • Discuss any recoding or transformations applied, and explain why these were necessary.
  • Include at least one visualisation that helps the reader understand the data (e.g., distributions, cross-tabulations, or bivariate relationships).

3. Model and results

  • Specify the regression model(s) you estimated and justify your modelling choices.
  • Present the regression output in a well-formatted table (e.g., using the modelsummary package or knitr::kable()).
  • Interpret the key coefficients in substantive terms. For example: “A one-unit increase in X is associated with a Y-unit change in the outcome, holding other variables constant.”
  • Discuss the overall model fit (e.g., R-squared for linear regression; pseudo R-squared for logistic regression).

4. Discussion and conclusion

  • Summarise your main findings in relation to your research question.
  • Discuss the limitations of your analysis (e.g., potential confounders, measurement issues, generalisability).
  • Suggest possible directions for further research.
NoteImportant

All code must be visible in your final PDF (echo: true). Your report must be fully reproducible — that is, it must render without errors from the .qmd source file and the data included in your project folder.

TipTip: Interpreting coefficients

Remember that regression coefficients from observational data should be interpreted as associations, not as causal effects. When discussing your results, use language such as “is associated with” or “predicts” rather than “causes” or “leads to”.

R programming tips

Importing data

Depending on your file format, you may need one of the following:

# CSV files
my_data <- read.csv("my_data.csv")

# SPSS files (.sav)
library(haven)
my_data <- read_sav("my_data.sav")

# Stata files (.dta)
library(haven)
my_data <- read_dta("my_data.dta")

# Excel files (.xlsx)
library(readxl)
my_data <- read_excel("my_data.xlsx")

Fitting a linear regression model

model_linear <- lm(outcome ~ var1 + var2 + var3,
                   data = my_data)
summary(model_linear)

Fitting a logistic regression model

If your outcome variable is binary (e.g., 1 = yes, 0 = no):

model_logistic <- glm(outcome ~ var1 + var2 + var3,
                      data = my_data,
                      family = binomial)
summary(model_logistic)

Presenting regression results in a table

The modelsummary package produces clean, publication-style regression tables:

library(modelsummary)

modelsummary(model_linear,
             stars = TRUE,
             title = "Predictors of outcome variable")

You can also compare multiple models side by side:

modelsummary(list("Model 1" = model1, "Model 2" = model2),
             stars = TRUE)
TipTip

Remember to load all required packages at the top of your .qmd document. You will likely need dplyr, ggplot2, and modelsummary, plus whichever package you need to import your data (e.g., haven, readxl, readr).
